The core logic

Organic acid–base chemistry is built on a surprisingly small set of rules. Internalize these five and the rest of the topic becomes pattern-matching.

  1. Reactions favor the weaker acid. An acid–base equilibrium always lies toward the side with the weaker acid — the one with the larger pKa. Equivalently, it favors the more stable (weaker) base.
  2. pKa = −log Ka. A smaller pKa means a stronger acid; a larger pKa means a weaker acid.
  3. Stronger acid ⇒ more stable conjugate base. To rank acids without a table, rank the stability of the anions they leave behind.
  4. Conjugate-base stability has a priority order: Atom (size & electronegativity) → Resonance → Inductive / proximity → Hybridization.
  5. Two skills are tested: deciding which way an equilibrium lies, and deciding which of two compounds is the stronger acid.

A quick illustration — deprotonating methanol with sodium hydride:

CH3–O–Hacid · pKa 16 + Na+ H−base ⇌ CH3–O− Na+conj. base + H2 ↑conj. acid · pKa 35

The reaction favors the right: it forms H2, the weaker acid (pKa 35 > 16). Hydride is a strong enough base to pull the O–H proton off, and the H2 gas bubbling away drives it further.

Section 01

Acid strength and pKa

Any acid H–A ionizes in water by handing a proton to a solvent molecule:

H–Aacid + H2Obase ⇌ A−conj. base + H3O+conj. acid

Because water is the solvent its concentration is essentially constant, so the equilibrium constant collapses into the acid dissociation constant:

Ka = [H3O+][A−] / [HA]   and   pKa = −log Ka

The farther to the right that ionization lies, the more H3O+ is produced, the larger Ka, and the stronger the acid.

★ Smaller pKa ⇒ stronger acid. Larger pKa ⇒ weaker acid. Each whole pKa unit is a factor of ten in acidity.

Section 03

The two exam questions

Nearly every acid–base problem is one of these two questions wearing a costume.

1 · Which way does the equilibrium lie?

If you have pKa values: the reaction favors the side with the weaker acid (the larger pKa).
If you don't: compare the structures of the two bases — the reaction favors the weaker, more stable base.

CH3CH2O–Hacid · pKa 16 + :NH2−base ⇌ CH3CH2O:−conj. base + NH3conj. acid · pKa 38

Favors the right — NH3 (pKa 38) is the weaker acid. Amide ion is a strong enough base to fully deprotonate ethanol.

2 · Which compound is the stronger acid?

If you have pKa: smaller pKa wins.
If you don't: the acid with the more stable conjugate base is stronger. That single idea — conjugate-base stability — is what the rest of this page is about.

Section 04

Ranking conjugate-base stability

When you remove a proton from H–A you create the anion A−. How stable that anion is decides how strong the acid was. Stability is governed by four factors, applied in priority order:

★ Sometimes remembered as ARIO — Atom, Resonance, Inductive, Orbital (hybridization). Check them top to bottom; the first factor that differs usually decides the winner.
A

Atom carrying the charge

Two sub-rules. Down a column (different rows of the periodic table), bigger atom = more stable anion — charge spreads over a larger volume. Across a row, the more electronegative atom = more stable anion. So I− beats Cl− (size), and CH3O− beats CH3NH− (electronegativity).

R

Resonance

More resonance structures = more stable. Spreading the negative charge over several atoms is far more stabilizing than parking it on one. This is why acetate (charge over two oxygens) is much more stable than methoxide (charge on one).

I

Inductive effect & proximity

Electronegative atoms pull electron density through the σ-bonds, draining charge away from the anion. More electronegative neighbors, more of them, and closer to the charge all increase stability. Fluorine helps more than bromine; two fluorines beat one; a fluorine one carbon away beats one three carbons away.

O

Orbital / hybridization

A lone pair in a smaller orbital sits closer to the nucleus and is held more tightly, so it is more stable. s-character shrinks the orbital: sp < sp² < sp³ in size, so an sp lone pair is the most stabilized. That makes a terminal alkyne C–H (pKa 25) far more acidic than an alkane C–H (pKa 50).

★ More stable conjugate base ⇒ stronger acid — and that same stability makes the base weaker (less reactive). Stability and basicity always pull in opposite directions.

Section 06

Resonance effects, in depth

Resonance is usually the most dramatic stabilizer on the list. Two classic comparisons:

Ethanol vs. acetic acid

CH3CH2–O–Hethanol · pKa 16 vs CH3–C(=O)–O–Hacetic acid · pKa 4.8 · stronger

Ethoxide (CH3CH2O−) traps its charge on a single oxygen. Acetate delocalizes the charge equally over two oxygens (CH3CO2− ⇌ CH3C(O−)=O), so it is far more stable — and acetic acid is ~11 pKa units stronger.

Propane vs. propene

CH3CH2CH3propane vs CH2=CH–CH3propene · stronger (allylic C–H)

Remove an allylic proton from propene and the resulting allyl anion is resonance-delocalized (CH2=CH–CH2− ⇌ −CH2–CH=CH2). The propyl anion from propane has no such escape, so propene is the stronger acid.

Section 07

Hybridization — and the basicity flip

For C–H acidity, more s-character means a smaller orbital, a more stable anion, and a more acidic proton:

H–C≡C–Hsp · most acidic > H2C=CH2sp² > CH3–CH3sp³ · least acidic

For bases, the trend reverses

A lone pair that is held tightly (lots of s-character) is stable — and a stable lone pair is a weak base. So among nitrogen bases, the sp³ lone pair is the stronger base, the sp lone pair the weakest.

★ Only electrons in p orbitals can be delocalized by resonance. A lone pair locked in an sp² orbital that points away from the π-system stays put — and a localized lone pair is a better base.

Pyridine vs. DMF

Pyridine is the stronger base. Its nitrogen lone pair sits in an sp² orbital in the plane of the ring, localized and available. In DMF [(CH3)2N–CHO] the nitrogen lone pair delocalizes into the carbonyl (R2N–C(=O) ⇌ R2N+=C–O−), spreading out the electrons.

Spreading out electrons ⇒ more stable ⇒ less basic. The same delocalization that stabilizes an anion also tames a lone pair's reactivity.
Section 08

Putting a number on it: Keq

For any acid–base reaction HA + B− ⇌ HB + A−, the equilibrium constant is just a ratio of the two acids' Ka values:

Keq = Ka(left acid) / Ka(right acid) = 10(pKa,right − pKa,left)

Can bicarbonate deprotonate phenol?

HCO3−base + C6H5–O–Hphenol · pKa 10 ⇌ H2CO3conj. acid · pKa 6.4 + C6H5–O−phenoxide

Keq = 10(−10) / 10(−6.4) = 10−3.6 ≈ 2.5 × 10−4. Since Keq < 1, the equilibrium lies left.

★ Answer: No. Bicarbonate can't appreciably deprotonate phenol — doing so would have to form the stronger acid H2CO3, which the equilibrium resists. (This is exactly why carboxylic acids, but not phenols, dissolve in aqueous bicarbonate — a classic separation trick.)

Section 10

Intermolecular forces & boiling point

A short companion topic. The strength of the forces between molecules sets physical properties like boiling point.

  • Van der Waals (London dispersion) forces act between temporary, induced dipoles — fleeting δ+/δ− regions in any molecule.
  • Larger, longer molecules (e.g. pentane) have more surface contact, so their dispersion forces are stronger.
  • Hydrogen bonding — an H attached to N, O, or F interacting with another N/O/F lone pair — is the strongest of the three.
van der Waals  <  dipole–dipole  <  hydrogen bonding
increasing IMF strength → increasing boiling point
Wrap-up

Quick reference — the logic chain

Everything on this page is one connected idea. Read it left to right:

small pKa⇔ strong acid⇔ stable conjugate base⇔ weak base

Conjugate-base stability is set, in order, by:
atom (size, electronegativity) → resonance → inductive / proximity → hybridization

An equilibrium always shifts toward the weaker acid (larger pKa) / more stable base.
Keq = Ka(left) / Ka(right) = 10(pKa,right − pKa,left)
